# GTM Strategy

## Exact Target User

The ideal early user is probably:

- An Engineering Manager or Founding Engineer
- At a 15-80 person startup where everyone started buying AI subscriptions independently and nobody really looked at the total cost until later.
- Already actively paying for multiple AI tools across the team

They often end up using multiple AI tools across the company:

- Cursor for devs
- ChatGPT Team for general usage
- Claude for writing/research
- Copilot because one team prefers it
- Gemini because someone wanted to try it

Over time, AI spend becomes fragmented and difficult to evaluate centrally. That’s usually the moment this product becomes useful.

# What They’re Searching Right Before They Need This

People usually start looking for something like BurnRateAI after:

- AI invoices suddenly jump
- finance asks what all these subscriptions are
- nobody knows which tools are actually worth paying for anymore

The searches are usually closer to:

- “how many AI subscriptions is too many AI subscriptions”
- “reduce SaaS spend”
- “AI tooling costs startup”
- “Cursor vs Copilot team pricing”
- “how much are startups spending on AI tools”

They’re also probably:
- scrolling X threads comparing AI tools
- reading Reddit discussions about stack choices
- looking at “What AI tools does your team use?” posts
- trying to justify yet another AI subscription internally

# First 100 Users With $0 Paid Budget

## 1. Post Real Audit Breakdowns

The easiest way to explain the product is to show the output.
We coulde create short posts showing:

- “This team was paying for 4 overlapping AI subscriptions”
- “Potential savings found: ₹18k/month”

without revealing company information.

## 2. Reply Marketing

Probably more effective than posting.

I’d reply to conversations like:
- “What AI tools does your team use?”
- “Cursor or Copilot?”
- “AI tooling costs are getting ridiculous”

with:
- mini audits
- screenshots
- pricing breakdowns
- actual optimization suggs


## 3. Shareable Report Loop

The public reports are intentionally designed to spread naturally.
When someone shares an audit link, it already contains:
- savings estimates
- recommendations
- branded preview cards
- visual breakdowns
So every shared report becomes a lightweight product demo.

## 4. Communities

I would manually share the tool inside:
- founder communities
- campus tech communities
- other channels

Not as “check out my startup”, but maybe as:
> “I built something to figure out where AI spend is getting wasted.”

That framing usually works better.

# The Unfair Distribution Channel

The strongest unfair advantage is turning audits themselves into shareable social objects.

Most SaaS spend tools generate:

- dashboards
- PDFs
- spreadsheets

BurnRateAI instead generates:

- visual reports
- clean preview cards
- shareable links

A good audit naturally creates:
- conversations
- screenshots
- shares

And thats what we want.

# What Week 1 Traction Looks Like

If the product resonates at all, realistic week-1 traction could look like:

- a few hundred visitors
- a decent number of completed audits
- some shared reports
- a handful of qualified leads
- people sending links around internally

The strongest signal would be:

- people sharing reports organically
- repeat discussions around AI tooling spend
- users sending audits internally within teams
